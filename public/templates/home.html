Hardly an area of law shows such imbalance between legislation and application as electronic signatures.
The killer application that would launch the use of such signature devices seem yet to be undiscovered.
1954
A rubber stamp was recognised as legally binding signature
The concept of Public Key Cryptography was introduced
1976
1981

1995

United Nations Commission on International Trade Law (UNCITRAL) Model Law on Electronic Commerce (MLEC) to ensure harmonious economic relations for United Nations members
1999
The European Union enacted the Directive on a Community Framework for Electronic Signatures to address member states uniformity
The US Uniform Transactions Act (UETA) to address state inconsistencies
The Australian Electronic Transactions Act (ETA) to ensure that Australian business has the opportunity to be at the forefront of electronic commerce
The US Electronic Signatures in Global and National Commerce Act (E-Sign) to ensure uniform across all US states (only 22 states had specific legislation)
